## The Take

Linear is a fantastic tool. For engineering teams. Sales teams using Linear as their CRM are flying blind on pipeline visibility, forecast accuracy, and deal velocity. The moment you start tracking actual revenue, Linear's task-management architecture becomes a liability, not a feature.

## Why Everyone Assumes Linear Works for Sales

It's easy to see why teams think Linear can double as a sales pipeline tool.

Linear is fast. The UI is genuinely delightful. It integrates with Slack. It has a free plan that doesn't nickel-and-dime you. And if you're already using it for product management, adding a "Sales" workspace feels natural. One tool. One workspace. One source of truth.

Plus, Linear has custom fields and statuses. You can technically create deal stages. You can attach a deal value to an issue. You can set custom fields for probability, close date, stakeholders. On paper, it looks like a CRM.

The problem is that Linear's entire architecture is built around task management, not revenue operations. It's optimized for shipping features on time, not for tracking pipeline health.

<Callout type="info" title="This matters if you're managing revenue">
  A sales pipeline and a product roadmap have opposite purposes. One needs to show you where money is coming from. The other shows you what work ships next. Linear solves the second problem brilliantly. It creates friction for the first.
</Callout>

## Where That Goes Wrong

Here's what happens when you force Linear to be your CRM:

**No deal rollups or forecast visibility.** Linear shows you what's in each deal (status, value, probability). It does not show you aggregate pipeline value by stage, by rep, or by month. You cannot see at a glance whether you're on track to hit quota. You have to manually add up all the deal values or export to a spreadsheet and rebuild the math yourself. That's not pipeline management. That's data entry.

**Single-contact tracking only.** Sales deals have multiple stakeholders. The VP of Ops approves. The Director of IT tests. Finance signs off. Linear treats each deal as a single issue tied to one account contact. If you need to track who's driving the deal versus who's the legal reviewer, you're adding custom fields and text notes. You cannot view a relationship map or multi-threaded conversation history without jumping to email or Slack.

**No sales-specific workflows.** Linear's status flow is issue-centric: Open, In Progress, Done. Sales needs different states: Discovery, Demo, Negotiation, Closed Won/Lost. Linear lets you rename these, but it has no native concept of deal stages that compress into weighted pipeline value. You're building a workaround, not using a system.

**Forecasting requires manual work.** A real CRM gives you forecast accuracy built in. You set probability by stage. The system calculates expected pipeline value. You can see upside, commit, and pipeline in one view. Linear gives you custom fields for probability. Then you either calculate by hand or use a formula field that doesn't actually integrate with your revenue targets. You end up exporting to Google Sheets anyway.

**No activity consolidation.** Calls, emails, notes, tasks. A sales CRM should show you the complete communication history for a deal in one place. Linear's activity feed shows issue comments and status changes. It doesn't pull in email threads, call notes, or meeting transcripts unless you manually paste them into a comment. Your deal context lives scattered across email, Slack, Google Drive, and Linear.

**Reporting is static.** Linear's analytics dashboard doesn't ask sales questions. It doesn't show deal velocity, win rate by stage, average close time, or rep productivity metrics. You get burndown charts. You get cycle time tracking. You get issue throughput. None of that tells you whether your pipeline is healthy or which deals are at risk.

## What Actually Works

A sales CRM needs to do three things Linear can't:

**Show you pipeline health at a glance.** When you open your CRM, you should see total pipeline by stage, weighted by probability, calculated automatically. Not a custom formula you built. Not a metric you pull from Sheets. Built in. Real tools like Pipedrive, HubSpot, Close, and Copper do this immediately. You land on the dashboard, see your pipeline, know whether you're on track.

**Track the whole relationship, not just one contact.** If your deal involves five people from the customer's side, your CRM should show all five, their titles, communication history, and influence level. Linear shows you one issue. That's it. You need a system that maps multi-threaded deals, showing who's been contacted, who's driving, who's blocking. Close and Copper both do this natively.

**Consolidate activity in one place.** Email should flow in automatically. Call logs should attach. Notes should live in the deal, not scattered across Slack and Google Drive. Pipedrive connects email and calendar. HubSpot has email tracking built in. Close and Copper have call recording and transcript integration. Linear has none of this. You're manually logging work, which means either under-logging or spending 20 minutes a day copying information around.

**Give you forecasting that updates in real time.** You set probability by stage once. The system calculates expected value. You drill into any forecast gap and see which deals are moving, which are stalled, which are at risk. HubSpot's free CRM does this. Pipedrive does this. Linear makes you recalculate every time you want to answer the question.

For teams with 3-8 reps and simple deal flows, Pipedrive or HubSpot's free plan will outperform Linear by weeks. For longer deal cycles with multi-threaded accounts, Close or Copper give you relationship mapping and activity consolidation that Linear simply doesn't have.

## The Real Cost of Using Linear for Sales

Let's be concrete about what this costs you.

**Time:** Every deal review becomes a manual exercise. You can't open a dashboard and see pipeline. You open Linear, navigate to your custom fields, maybe export to Sheets, recalculate probability, and manually add up the numbers. For a team of 5 reps reviewing 50 deals, that's 2-3 hours a week you're not spending on selling or qualification.

**Forecast accuracy:** You're not tracking the right metrics. Linear doesn't show you deal velocity by stage or average close time. You don't know if a deal sitting in Demo for 45 days is normal or a red flag. You don't know your actual win rate by stage, so your probability estimates drift over time. This cascades into inaccurate forecasts and surprised leadership every quarter.

**Deal slippage:** With no activity consolidation or multi-contact tracking, deals fall through cracks. You talk to one person, think the deal is progressing, but three other stakeholders haven't actually engaged. You don't have a system pushing back to check on the VP of Ops or get Finance engaged. You find out the deal slipped to next quarter during a forecast review, not during qualification.

**Sales-to-leadership conversations:** Your CEO asks, "What's at risk in the next 30 days?" You have no answer ready. You have to build a report. Linear doesn't give you a risk view. You're scrambling while other teams hand their forecast to leadership in 30 seconds.

**Onboarding and scaling:** Your first sales hire understands Linear. Your third hire doesn't know that you've renamed issues to deals and custom fields to stages. They log information inconsistently or skip it. Data quality tanks. By the time you realize you need to migrate off Linear, you've built 300 "deal issues" in a system that was never designed to hold them.

For a $2M ARR company losing 15-20% of forecast accuracy from pipeline blindness, that's real money. And you're doing it to save $99 a month on Pipedrive.

## Who Should Still Use Linear for Sales

There are two exceptions.

**Very small teams (1-2 reps) doing very simple deals.** If you have a founder and one sales hire selling a straightforward product with a 2-3 week cycle and one decision-maker per customer, Linear might be okay as a placeholder. You can move a deal from Outreach to Demo to Closed in Linear and get the gist. The moment you have three reps, longer cycles, or multiple stakeholders per deal, Linear stops working. Move before you're forced to.

**Teams that are already deeply integrated with Linear for product work and have very short sales cycles.** If your company runs on Linear for engineering and you're selling a $500 deal with a one-week cycle, the friction of switching systems might outweigh the benefits. But be honest: is that really your situation? Most B2B sales teams aren't selling in one week.

For everyone else: Linear is a distraction. It feels like you're being efficient because the tool is beautiful and fast. But efficiency is shipping deals faster and knowing your pipeline health. Linear gives you neither.

## The Bottom Line

Linear is built to optimize feature delivery. Sales needs to optimize revenue visibility. These are not the same problem, and a great product-management tool makes a mediocre sales tool.

If you're using Linear for sales, you're not saving money. You're paying for the privilege of manually calculating what a real CRM would show you automatically. Move to Pipedrive, HubSpot, Close, or Copper depending on your deal complexity. You'll cut deal review time in half, see forecast risks before they surprise you, and actually know whether your pipeline is healthy.

The cost is a few hours of migration. The upside is weeks of better visibility every quarter. That's a trade worth making.

## The Take

ChatGPT Teams costs $30 per user per month. Most teams buying it assume they need it because everyone else has it. They don't. For operations managers, founders, and research-heavy teams, Perplexity Teams and Claude Teams deliver more value at lower cost, and your team's actual workflow probably aligns better with one of them than with ChatGPT's feature set. This isn't contrarian cheerleading. This is about aligning spend to actual work.

## Why Everyone Assumes ChatGPT Teams Is The Standard

ChatGPT hit the mainstream first. It owns brand recognition. When a founder tells their team "get ChatGPT Pro," the team upgrades. When they scale to five people, ChatGPT Teams feels like the natural next step.

The marketing story is also loud. ChatGPT Teams emphasizes the 200K token context window (the longest in the game), native Canvas for structured editing, and GPT-4o's reasoning capability. The feature list reads like the safest choice: if you go with ChatGPT, nobody questions your decision.

Enterprise vendors (Microsoft for Copilot Pro, Google for Gemini Business) also push the narrative that AI assistants are interchangeable. They're not. But the default assumption is: more features, higher price, must be better.

What gets lost in that logic is the actual work your team does. Most small operations teams don't need 200K tokens. They need research speed, clear citations, and shared workspaces. Those are different products.

<Callout type="info" title="What actually matters for team adoption">
  Price per user matters less than whether the tool fits your workflow. A $20/month tool your team uses 10 times a day beats a $30/month tool used once a week. ChatGPT Teams wins on perception, not on per-dollar utility for most small teams.
</Callout>

## Where That Goes Wrong

ChatGPT Teams has real limitations that only show up after you're paying for it.

First: the research problem. ChatGPT Teams requires a paid Web search add-on ($20/month per organization, on top of the per-seat cost) to access current information. Your team paying $30/seat for ChatGPT Teams is actually paying $30/seat plus a $20 org fee to do what Perplexity Teams does out of the box. By seat six, you're overpaying.

Second: the context window feature is marketing, not utility. ChatGPT Teams advertises 200K tokens. Great for uploading a 100-page document and asking analytical questions. But most small teams don't upload documents that large. They upload 5-20 page PDFs, research summaries, or Notion exports. Claude Teams handles 200K tokens too. Perplexity handles research citations better than either. You're paying for a feature that sits unused.

Third: shared workspaces. ChatGPT Teams has them, but they're weak. You get access to shared conversations, but no admin controls for who can see what. Claude Teams and the newer Perplexity Teams both have granular workspace management. If compliance or data access matters to you (and it should), ChatGPT Teams is actually the riskier choice.

Fourth: cost scales poorly with team size. At five people, ChatGPT Teams is $150/month plus $20 for web search. Claude Teams is $150/month with no add-ons. Perplexity Teams pricing starts lower ($15/month per seat for the standard tier, $20 for Pro features). At 10 people, ChatGPT costs $320 (with web search). Perplexity costs $150-200. Claude costs $300. The math shifts hard.

## What Actually Works

Let's map the three by real workflow:

**Perplexity Teams** is built for research. Your operations team wants to understand a competitor's pricing changes, build an industry report, or fact-check claims. Perplexity gives you web search, real-time data, and citations tied to sources. You see where the information came from. That's $15-20 per seat, and it's the fastest way to close a research question. Most operations and sales teams spend 30-40% of their time researching. Perplexity is purpose-built for this. If your team does research, start here.

**Claude Teams** is better for internal analysis and structured drafting. Your marketing manager is building a case study. Your product ops manager is analyzing user feedback from Notion or Slack. Claude excels at understanding long context and generating coherent, long-form output. It also has better safety guardrails for handling sensitive customer data. At $30 per seat (minimum 5 users), Claude Teams makes sense for teams that draft, analyze internal docs, and need strong data privacy. The admin controls are also tighter than ChatGPT's.

**ChatGPT Teams** wins only if your team does specialized reasoning work that requires extended context and you already have the budget. Design teams building complex UI systems. Product teams analyzing hundreds of user interviews at once. Research teams working with huge datasets. These workflows benefit from the 200K window and Canvas. But most 5-50 person teams don't fit this profile.

The practical move: audit your actual work. Track what tasks your team runs through an AI assistant for two weeks. Count research tasks, drafting tasks, and analysis tasks. If research is more than 25% of the work, Perplexity wins on price and capability. If it's internal analysis and drafting, Claude wins on quality and control. ChatGPT only wins if you need the context window or you're already locked into the ecosystem for another reason (like API integrations).

## The Real Cost of Overpaying for ChatGPT Teams

Let's put numbers on the waste.

A 10-person team on ChatGPT Teams spends:
- $300/month in seat costs
- $20/month for web search
- $3,840 per year

The same team on Perplexity Teams (Pro tier at $20/seat) spends:
- $200/month
- $2,400 per year

The same team on Claude Teams spends:
- $300/month (5-user minimum, so actually less waste if you're under 5)
- $3,600 per year

ChatGPT costs $1,440 more per year than Perplexity. That's real money for a 10-person team.

But that's not the full cost. There's also the cost of unused features. If your team isn't running tasks that need a 200K context window or Canvas, you're paying a premium on a feature set you don't use. That's behavioral waste.

And there's switching cost. If you sign your whole team up on ChatGPT Teams, moving later (even if you realize it's not the right fit) means re-establishing workflows, re-training on a new interface, and losing conversation history. ChatGPT's network effect keeps you locked in even after the economic case breaks down.

<Notice>
  If your team uses ChatGPT Teams mainly for web search, you're subsidizing features you don't need. Audit a month of actual usage. Count the percentage of conversations that need Canvas, 200K context, or advanced reasoning. If it's under 20%, you're overpaying.
</Notice>

## Who Should Still Use ChatGPT Teams the Old Way

This isn't an argument that ChatGPT Teams is bad. It's an argument that it's wrong for most teams.

Use ChatGPT Teams if:

You're building complex systems that require sustained reasoning across hundreds of pages of context. Enterprise architecture decisions, deep technical analysis, research synthesis across dozens of sources. The 200K window and GPT-4o's reasoning capability matter here.

You're already locked into OpenAI's ecosystem for API work or custom GPTs. If your product team built a GPT-based customer support bot or an internal automation, ChatGPT Teams integrates tighter than alternatives.

Your team specializes in creative writing, long-form content generation, or code generation at scale. GPT-4o is very strong here. If that's 50%+ of your work, the cost makes sense.

You have budget constraints that aren't about money. You need a single vendor relationship for compliance or procurement reasons. Some enterprises require a single AI vendor contract. ChatGPT Teams has that advantage.

But if you're buying ChatGPT Teams because it's the default, or because you heard it was the best, or because you don't want to waste time evaluating, you're making an expensive assumption.

The comparison you should actually run is based on work type, not brand. ## The Bottom Line

You're not overpaying for ChatGPT because it's a bad product. You're overpaying because you haven't matched the tool to the work. ChatGPT Teams is built for a specific use case (long-context reasoning, structured output, creative generation) that most small teams don't live in. Perplexity Teams is built for research speed and accuracy. Claude Teams is built for internal analysis and drafting with strong controls.

The move: Spend two weeks tracking your team's actual AI usage. Count research sessions, drafting sessions, and analysis sessions. Then pick the tool that fits the split. You'll save $1,000-2,000 a year and probably get better results because the tool will match the work.

Start with Perplexity Teams if research is significant. Start with Claude Teams if you're analyzing internal documents and need privacy controls. Only pick ChatGPT Teams if you genuinely need the context window or Canvas. Anything else is a brand decision wearing a productivity costume.

## Skip This If You Already Know What You Need

- **If you have 5-10 people and minimal structure** — use Slite. Cheap, fast, built for small teams, good search.
- **If you're using Jira or Confluence already** — Confluence solves this, but you'll pay $10/user/month. Only worth it if you need deep integration with Jira.
- **If you need offline-first or self-hosted** — Bookstack or Gitbook (for technical teams). Everyone else should skip the maintenance hassle.

## What Actually Matters When Choosing a Wiki Tool

A wiki is only valuable if people actually use it. That means you need to evaluate tools on five specific dimensions, not on feature lists.

**Search speed and relevance.** Your wiki dies if people can't find what they need in three seconds. A 25-person team will accumulate 200+ documents fast. If search returns 40 results when someone asks "onboarding," they'll give up and email someone instead. Test the search on a real scenario before you commit.

**Permission granularity without complexity.** You need to share some docs company-wide and keep others private (payroll, hiring, sales pipeline). Tools that require fiddling with permission dialogs for every document kill adoption. You want role-based access (e.g., "everyone reads, only ops writes") that works in bulk.

**Speed and reliability.** If your wiki takes 3 seconds to load a page or crashes when you paste a screenshot, people will default to email and Slack. This matters more than features. A slow tool is a dead tool.

**Comment and collaboration workflow.** You need a way to ask questions on docs without creating a Slack thread that evaporates in two days. This is where wiki tools beat folders of Google Docs.

**Integration with your stack.** A wiki that doesn't connect to your CRM, task manager, or Slack is more friction than it's worth. You'll spend time copying information instead of building once.

<Callout type="info" title="The Notion trap">
  Notion works until it doesn't. At 15+ people with 300+ pages, load times climb to 5+ seconds, and the permission model forces you to make everything shared or nothing shared. You'll eventually rebuild in a dedicated wiki tool and regret the six months you spent on Notion.
</Callout>

## Slite: The Right Choice When...

You have 5-20 people, need the tool live in a week, and don't want to configure anything. Slite is a wiki designed for speed. Pages load in under a second, search works, and permissions work like you'd expect (you can share a folder with a team). It costs $8/user/month on annual billing, which puts a 10-person team at $80/month. That's half the price of Notion and it's faster. The weak spot is integrations, limited API, and you can't build the complex document relations that Notion allows. But you don't need those things if you're building an internal knowledge base. Slite works best for teams that prioritize getting started over building a custom system.

## Confluence: The Right Choice When...

You're already deep in the Atlassian ecosystem. If your team uses Jira for bug tracking or sprints, Confluence integration makes sense because your engineers can jump from a bug to related documentation in one click. At 10 people, Confluence costs $10/user/month, which makes it the most expensive option here. You're paying for the Jira relationship, not for Confluence itself being better at wikis. Confluence is also the only option here where you can build sophisticated permission models tied to Jira projects. If you don't use Jira, pick something else.

## GitBook: The Right Choice When...

You're building a technical knowledge base or product documentation that you might want to publish to customers later. GitBook is built on Git, which means engineers will understand it immediately. It supports Markdown, versioning, and change tracking. It also costs nothing if you use it as a public site, and $8/user/month for private spaces. The catch: GitBook assumes you're writing technical documentation. It's awkward for operational guides, HR policies, or sales playbooks. If your wiki is 60% technical specs and 40% HR stuff, GitBook still works but it feels like forcing it. Best for engineering-first teams.

## Bookstack: The Right Choice When...

You need self-hosted infrastructure or you have compliance constraints that forbid cloud storage. Bookstack is free, open-source, and runs on your own server. You get full control and zero monthly bills. The trade-off: you need to manage a server (or hire someone to), there's no built-in collaboration workflow (comments are basic), and the UI feels dated. It works for teams smaller than 10 that have a technical founder willing to set it up. For everyone else, the support burden outweighs the $80-100/month you'd save.

## Tettra: The Right Choice When...

You want AI-powered search and you're willing to try something newer. Tettra was designed to solve the "I don't know where to find this" problem. It uses AI to understand what someone is asking for and surface the right doc, even if they don't use the exact terminology. At $10/user/month, it costs the same as Notion, but it's faster and purpose-built for knowledge bases. The risk: Tettra is still smaller than Slite or Confluence, so the community is smaller and the feature roadmap is less predictable. Test it for a month before committing.

<Notice>
  Tettra's AI search is useful but not magic. It still requires your team to write clear documentation. Garbage in, garbage out.
</Notice>

## Red Flags to Watch Out For

**Per-seat pricing that scales badly.** If a wiki tool charges $10/user/month and you're at 30 people, that's $3,600/year. At 50 people, it's $6,000/year. Compare that to a flat-rate tool like Bookstack ($0) or a tool with unlimited users like Gitbook ($8/user but capped). Look for the per-user costs hiding in the fine print.

**Free plans that disappear when you need them.** GitBook's free plan is great until you want to invite your second team. Notion's free plan works until you hit the page limit. Test with your actual team size before committing.

**Search that doesn't work on day one.** Before you sign up, ask the vendor for a demo where they search for something on their own instance. If search takes more than 2 seconds, move on.

**Integrations that are "on the roadmap."** If the tool you want doesn't integrate with your CRM or task manager yet, assume it never will. Build your decision on what exists, not what might exist.

**Permission models that require clicking through every document.** If you can't bulk-share a folder with a team, the tool is designed for small teams and you'll outgrow it in six months.

**Vendor lock-in without an export option.** Make sure you can export your docs as Markdown or PDF. If the vendor shuts down or raises prices, you shouldn't be stuck.

## My Pick for Most Teams

**Slite for 5-25 people, Confluence for teams already using Jira, GitBook for technical teams.**

If you're between 5 and 25 people and don't have Jira, start with Slite. It's cheap, it's fast, and it gets out of your way. You won't build a 10-layer hierarchy of folders or get lost in permission configuration. You'll write docs and people will find them. At $8/user/month on annual billing, a team of 15 costs $120/month. Compare that to Notion at $10/user/month ($150/month) or Confluence at $10/user/month ($150/month). Slite saves money and is faster.

If you outgrow Slite (which takes 18+ months for most teams), you'll know it. You'll hit the integration limits or need more advanced permissions. That's the time to move to Confluence or a self-hosted solution. But you'll have a year of actual usage data that tells you what you need.

The real mistake small teams make is treating the wiki choice like a one-time permanent decision. It's not. Treat it like a two-year commitment with a quarterly review. Pick something that works now, measure if people use it, and migrate if the cost or friction becomes unreasonable.

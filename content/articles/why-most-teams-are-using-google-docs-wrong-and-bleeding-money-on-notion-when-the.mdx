## The Take

Your team doesn't need Notion. It needs to stop treating Google Docs like a dump folder and start using basic information architecture. Most small teams spend $150-300 per year per person on Notion subscriptions while their actual workflows stay broken. They write documents with zero naming convention, share via scattered Drive links, and then blame the tool for chaos that better Docs habits would fix. Then they hear "Notion is better for knowledge management" and sign up for the paid tier, repeat the same mistakes, and wonder why onboarding still takes weeks.

The issue isn't the tool. It's the workflow.

## Why Everyone Assumes Notion Is the Answer

Notion got smart marketing. It positioned itself as the "all-in-one workspace" for teams. One place for docs, databases, tasks, wikis, and roadmaps. For founders and ops managers who've been frustrated watching Google Drive become a black hole, Notion looks like salvation.

Google Docs, by comparison, looks like a relic. It's basic. No databases. No custom properties. No rollups. No inline tasks. If you need to build anything beyond a flat document, you're stuck.

Notion also solves a real pain point: Google Drive's search and organization are genuinely bad. Try finding a decision from six months ago in a folder with 200 nested Drive links. You'll fail. Notion sells a better way: centralized, searchable, connected.

The issue is that Notion is solving a problem most small teams don't actually have yet. And the cost of building a Notion workspace properly is higher than most founders admit.

## Where That Goes Wrong

Here's what happens in practice:

A 10-person ops team pays $150-200/month for Notion. Someone creates a main workspace. They dump in databases for everything: projects, meeting notes, processes, roadmaps, client info, internal wiki. Six weeks later, half the databases are empty because the team didn't adopt it. The other half are outdated because updates require context switching through multiple views. Meeting notes pile up in Notion while Slack messages stay in Slack because jumping between tools breaks flow. The promised "single source of truth" never materializes.

Meanwhile, Google Docs is free (as part of Google Workspace). It integrates seamlessly with Gmail, Calendar, and Gmail. Comments, suggestions, and real-time collaboration work instantly. Permissions are straightforward. New team members already know how to use it.

The real cost isn't just the Notion subscription. It's the onboarding tax. Someone on the team (usually an operator or founder) spends 30-60 hours building templates, setting up database properties, creating views, and documenting how to use it. Then they spend another 20 hours each quarter maintaining it as the workflow changes. That's real work. Google Docs requires almost no setup beyond a folder structure and a naming convention.

<Callout type="info" title="The time cost is invisible">
  A Notion setup often takes 40-80 hours of owner time across the first two months. That's 1-2 weeks of part-time work that compounds over time. Meanwhile, a Google Docs system with clear folder structure and naming convention takes 4-6 hours to establish and almost zero maintenance.
</Callout>

For context: if you're paying yourself or your team lead $50/hour, 60 hours of Notion setup is $3,000 in real cost, on top of the subscription. You're not getting that back unless your Notion workspace actually reduces meeting time or accelerates decisions. Usually, it doesn't.

Google Docs also has a critical advantage: it's harder to overthink. You can't build 15 database views or get lost customizing properties. You write the document, you share it, you iterate. The constraint forces clarity.

Notion's flexibility is also its weakness. A 12-person team can spend weeks debating the "right" database schema for their product roadmap, only to realize it doesn't map to how they actually think about priorities. Google Docs doesn't let you have that conversation. You just write it in plain English.

## What Actually Works

Stop confusing "documentation tool" with "workspace platform." They're different problems.

For 80% of small teams (5-30 people), the right stack is simpler:

**Google Docs for writing and sharing.** One folder per project or functional area. Clear naming: `[Status] Project Name [Owner]` or `[Date] [Type] Title`. Use the outline feature to organize long documents. Share links only from the folder, not individual docs scattering across email. Make the folder the source of truth, not the doc.

**Google Sheets for lightweight data.** Need to track status across 20 projects? Use a sheet with filters and sorting. No databases, no custom properties. You already know how to use it. Add one column for status, one for owner, one for deadline. Done.

**Google Forms for intake.** Collecting ideas, feedback, or client requests? Use a form that auto-populates a sheet. Link to it from Docs. When the issue is resolved, move that row to an "Archive" sheet.

**Folders with clear structure.** Keep it simple: `[Year] Company Stuff` > `[Function]` > `[Project]`. Use the search function (it actually works for recent docs). Archive old folders yearly.

**A single shared doc as the operating manual.** Not a database. One document: "How We Work." It covers folder structure, naming convention, decision-making process, meeting cadence, who owns what. Link to it from your shared Drive folder. Update it once a quarter.

This approach costs you nothing (Google Workspace is $6-18/user/month anyway), requires one afternoon to set up, and scales to 30+ people without breaking.

When do you graduate to Notion? When you need **true database functionality**: filtering projects by multiple properties, linking roadmap items to goals to OKRs, building custom views for different stakeholders, or creating a product information architecture that feeds multiple downstream tools.

If you're managing a product roadmap with 50+ items, dependencies between them, and stakeholders who need different views (engineering sees by sprint, product sees by goal, leadership sees by quarter), Notion makes sense. If you're tracking three internal processes and a project folder, it doesn't.

## The Real Cost of Notion When You Don't Need It

Let's quantify this for a 10-person team using Notion but not actually needing it:

**Direct costs:** $150-200/month for Notion Team plan = $1,800-2,400/year.

**Indirect costs:** 50 hours of setup and maintenance across the year = $2,500 at $50/hour.

**Adoption friction:** Two team members spend 10 hours each learning Notion features they'll never use = $1,000.

**Opportunity cost:** Processes that could be documented in Docs stay vague because "we should put it in Notion" = delayed onboarding for new hires, repeated Q&A work.

Total first-year cost for a 10-person team: **$5,300-6,000 for a tool that's likely less efficient than what you already have.**

Compare that to Google Docs: $1,200-1,800/year (Google Workspace) + 4 hours of setup ($200) = **$1,400-2,000**. And it works better for collaborative writing.

The Notion argument wins when you're big enough and specialized enough that the database functionality actually saves you time daily. A product team with 30 linked epics, a design system database, and a roadmap that queries both saves hours per week using Notion. A 10-person consulting firm tracking clients, projects, and contract terms probably doesn't.

## Who Should Still Use It the Old Way

Notion makes sense for:

**Product teams building complex dependencies.** If your roadmap is a relational network (features connect to goals connect to OKRs connect to quarterly plans), Notion's database linking saves you hours weekly. Google Sheets can't model that.

**Operations teams managing multiple interconnected processes.** If you're tracking hiring pipeline, vendor relationships, financial forecasts, and contract terms, and they need to connect to each other, Notion's relational database beats a folder of Docs.

**Design or content teams with version control and asset management.** If you're managing client deliverables, approval workflows, or asset libraries, Notion's database structure plus integrations with tools like Zapier can be more efficient than Docs.

**Teams larger than 25-30 people.** At that scale, Google Drive's search and folder structure start to break. Notion's centralized, searchable workspace becomes valuable.

But here's the key: if you're in the "should use Notion" camp, you'll know it because you're already feeling the pain of a flat document structure. You're not guessing. You're migrating away from Docs because Docs is actually failing you.

For most small teams, the pain you're feeling is a Docs problem, not a tool problem. The folder is disorganized. The naming convention is inconsistent. There's no definition of who owns what. Notion won't fix any of that because it's not Notion's job.

<Notice>
  If you've been using Notion for six months and still default to Docs for writing or using a spreadsheet for tracking because "it's faster," you made the wrong choice. The tool should reduce friction, not add it.
</Notice>

## The Bottom Line

You probably don't need Notion yet. What you need is a 30-minute conversation about folder structure and a one-page guide on naming conventions. Share that guide from your Drive, build muscle memory, and then reassess in six months.

If you do have Notion, ask yourself: Am I using the database linking features multiple times per week? Do I actually need rollups, filters, and relations across documents? Or am I just writing docs in Notion because I already paid for it? If it's the latter, export to Docs, cancel Notion, and spend that $2,400/year on something you actually need.

For most ops managers, founders, and small teams: start with Google Docs, a clear folder structure, and a five-minute rule about naming. When that genuinely breaks, move to Notion. Don't move preemptively because marketing convinced you that "all-in-one workspace" is better. It's not. Focused tools that work are better.

Your documentation problem isn't a software problem. It's a discipline problem.
